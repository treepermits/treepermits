How to appeal a tree removal decision?

Would you like to challenge a decision to remove trees on one of the properties listed on this site? Take a look at the deadline to file an appeal — if it still hasn't passed, you can do it!

If you need help, join our WhatsApp chat — we can help you file the appeal.
1
Figure out who can file the appeal
  • Are you the next-door neighbor? Great — you'll pay a reduced fee to file.
  • Not next-door? Can you find the next-door neighbor who'd like to file and appear at City Hall when called?
  • Do you live within 500ft and there's an HOA? The HOA can file for a reduced fee.
  • No HOA but within 500ft? You can still apply, but you'll pay an extra $200.
  • None of the above? Text us in the chat — we will be able to help you submit the appeal for a reduced fee.
2
Request the invoice by email
Locate the Decision Application Number (looks like BD25-000000-001) and the address from the table above. Then send an email to PZHearingBoards@miami.gov as soon as possible:
If you are NOT the next-door neighbor within 500ft:
My name is (NAME) and I am requesting the invoice for the appeal of the Intended Decision Application No. (NUMBER). The subject property address is (ADDRESS).

If you ARE the next-door neighbor:
My name is (NAME) and I am requesting the invoice for the appeal of the Intended Decision Application No. (NUMBER) as an ABUTTING PROPERTY OWNER. The subject property address is (ADDRESS).

If you are a NON-PROFIT:
My name is (NAME) and I am representing not-for-profit organization (NAME OF ORG) requesting the invoice for the appeal of the Intended Decision Application No. (NUMBER) as a NON-PROFIT. The subject property address is (ADDRESS).
You will receive the invoice the same day.
3
Pay the fees
There is a number on the invoice you received. Go to miami.gov/make-a-payment, enter your invoice number, and pay the fees.

Save the receipt! You will need to submit it later.
4
Download the screenshot of radius and the list of residents within 500ft

5
Complete the property owner affirmation form
Print this form. Write in pen the number of "property owners shown in the attached Excel file" (the count from your list). Sign, scan, and save with the date and property address in the filename.
6
Write your appeal letter

▶ Example 1 — Nonprofit organization (1837 NE 4 Ave)

APPEAL OF INTENDED DECISION
Application No.: BD24-022585-001
Property Address: 1837 NE 4 Avenue, Miami, Florida
Date Issued: April 7, 2026

To: Preservation Officer and Hearing Boards, City of Miami Environmental Resources

Dear Preservation Officer and Members of the Hearing Board,

Pursuant to the Code of the City of Miami, Florida, as amended (the "Code"), this letter constitutes a formal appeal by the Sierra Club Miami Group, a nonprofit corporation dedicated to the conservation and protection of the natural and physical environment, of the Intended Decision issued on April 7, 2026, regarding Application No. BD24-022585-001, which proposes the removal of four (4) specimen-sized Mahogany trees located within the public right-of-way in front of 1837 NE 4 Avenue. This appeal is timely filed prior to the April 17, 2026 deadline.

Basis for Appeal: Removal of Specimen / Legacy Trees in the Public Right-of-Way

The application proposes removal of four (4) specimen-sized Mahogany trees, which are among the most valuable and protected species in Miami's urban forest. These trees are located within the public right-of-way, meaning they are a shared public asset, not solely subject to private development interests. Under the Code, specimen and protected trees, particularly state-threatened native species such as Mahogany, warrant heightened protection due to their ecological, aesthetic, and community value.

The stated justification for removal, "trees are in conflict with the proposed construction," does not, on its own, meet the threshold required under the Code to justify removal of protected or specimen trees.

The Code requires: demonstration that no reasonable design alternatives exist; consideration of tree preservation as a priority; exploration of site plan modifications to avoid or minimize impacts. There is no evidence provided that alternative site designs were meaningfully evaluated, that construction plans were adjusted to preserve these trees, or that root protection, setbacks, or redesign options were exhausted. Tree removal for convenience or maximization of development footprint is inconsistent with the intent of the Code.

While the application proposes seven (7) replacement trees and a contribution to the Tree Trust Fund, this does not adequately compensate for the loss of mature, specimen-sized Mahogany trees. Mature trees provide exponentially greater canopy coverage, carbon sequestration, and cooling benefits. Replacement trees will take decades to provide equivalent ecological value. Tree Trust Fund contributions do not replace the localized environmental and community benefits of existing canopy. This results in a net loss of canopy, contrary to the City's resilience and sustainability goals.

Based on the foregoing, we respectfully request that the Preservation Officer and Hearing Board: (1) deny the permit for removal of the four (4) specimen-sized Mahogany trees; or (2) in the alternative, require a revised site plan that preserves the trees, independent arborist review and verification, and full compliance with all Code provisions prioritizing preservation over removal.

Thank you for your consideration.
Submitted by: Sierra Club Miami Group

▶ Example 3 — Abutting property owner (3834 El Prado Blvd)

March 13, 2026

To: City of Miami – Planning Department, Office of Hearing Boards, 444 SW 2nd Avenue, Miami, FL 33130
Cc: Preservation Officer, City of Miami

Subject: Appeal of Intended Decision – Tree Removal Permit, Application BD23-029149-001, Property: 3834 El Prado Blvd

Dear Preservation Officer and Members of the Hearing Boards,

I hereby submit an appeal regarding the Intended Decision dated March 3, 2026 concerning the removal, relocation, and replacement of numerous trees at 3834 El Prado Boulevard. I reside directly across from the subject property and am therefore an abutting property owner directly affected by the proposed tree removals. I respectfully request that this appeal be scheduled for review before the City of Miami Hearing Boards.

Coconut Grove is widely recognized for its exceptional tree canopy and mature vegetation, which are essential to the historic character, environmental resilience, and quality of life of the neighborhood. The proposed activity includes the removal or relocation of numerous established trees on the property, including canopy and ornamental species such as Gumbo Limbo, Avocado, Sapodilla, Ylang-Ylang, Frangipani, Florida Boxwood, Citrus, Bottlebrush, Crape Myrtle, and several palms.

The notice indicates 15 tree removals and 5 tree relocations. This represents a significant level of tree removal for a property of this size. The justification cited for the removals is a "conflict with the proposed construction." However, development plans should reasonably adapt to existing natural features whenever feasible. Approving the removal of mature trees primarily to maximize the building envelope of a new residence risks undermining the long-standing environmental character of Coconut Grove.

Chapter 17 of the City of Miami Code establishes clear protections and policy objectives regarding the preservation of trees and the maintenance of the city's tree canopy. The intent of these provisions is to preserve mature trees whenever possible and to ensure that development is designed in a manner that minimizes unnecessary removal.

Given the scale of the proposed removals and the importance of preserving Coconut Grove's tree canopy, I respectfully request that this decision be reviewed and reconsidered, and that alternatives be explored that would preserve a greater portion of the existing trees on the property. A number of neighboring residents share these concerns and have expressed their intention to formally support this appeal.

Thank you for your consideration.
Sincerely, an abutting property owner

▶ Example 4 — Attorney on behalf of resident (3101 Grand Ave)

DAVID J. WINKER, P.A.
4720 S. LeJeune Rd, Coral Gables, FL 33146

Formal Appeal: Tree Removal Permit – 3101 GRAND AVE
Process Number # BD25021627001

City of Miami Preservation Officer and Members of the Hearing Boards:

I am writing to formally appeal the City's approval of the removal of 48 trees and the relocation of 3 Live Oaks at 3101 Grand Ave. I represent a resident of the area, who is filing this appeal over concerns about the environmental impact to the community on behalf of many community members. This decision violates the many laws and codes meant to prevent such an excessive removal of trees. This decision has been met with huge public outcry from residents.

In the Citizen's Bill of Rights: "The City shall promote the right of the people to clean air, pure water, freedom from excessive and unnecessary noise, and the natural, scenic, historic and aesthetic qualities of the environment." Further, it provides that "It shall be the policy of the City to conserve and protect its natural resources and scenic beauty…."

Under Miami 21 Article 9.1, the City has a duty to "Prevent the destruction of the City's existing tree canopy and promote its expansion" and to "Promote the use of trees and shrubs for energy conservation by encouraging cooling through the provision of shade and the channeling of breezes, thereby helping to offset global warming and local heat island effects through the added absorption of carbon dioxide and reduction of heat islands."

Chapter 17, Sec. 17-1 states: "The intent of this article is to protect, preserve and restore the tree canopy within the City of Miami by regulating the removal, relocation, pruning, planting and trimming of trees. The purpose of this article is to assure that the design and construction of all development activity is executed in a manner consistent with the preservation of existing trees to the greatest extent possible."

The removal of these trees would be antithetical to these code provisions as the trees do not unreasonably restrict the permitted use of the property. Further, their removal will lead to the erosion of health of residents, creation of heat islands and drive property values down.

Sincerely,
David J. Winker, Esq., on behalf of a community member

▶ Example 5 — Abutting property owner (3151 SW 27 Ave)

May 21, 2026

To: City of Miami – Planning Department, Office of Hearing Boards, 444 SW 2nd Avenue, Miami, FL 33130
Cc: Preservation Officer, City of Miami

Subject: Appeal of Intended Decision – Tree Removal Permit, Application BD25-028988-001, Property: 3151 SW 27 AV

Dear Preservation Officer and Members of the Hearing Boards,

I hereby submit an appeal regarding the Intended Decision dated May 12, 2026 concerning the removal of numerous trees at 3151 SW 27 AV (lots 3151, 3163, 3167). I reside directly across from the subject property and am therefore an abutting property owner affected by the proposed tree removals. I respectfully request that this appeal be scheduled for review before the City of Miami Hearing Boards.

The proposed activity includes the removal of 26 non-prohibited trees and 15 palms and no relocations. The trees include 13 natives (7 Gumbo Limbo, 4 Live Oak, 1 Strangler Fig, 1 Orange Geiger), 8 Tropical Almond, 1 Royal Poinciana, 1 Madrus Thorn, 1 Pongam, 2 Bridalveil. Most of these, along with giant Royal Palms, are mature and established. Many trees are situated along the fence or close to the side of the property and could potentially be considered for preservation even with construction of substantial footprint.

The intended decision states that replacements will include 13 hardwoods within the lot and 4 palms. 3 hardwoods will be planted in right-of-way, and "the remainder will be planted within City Parks/Properties within District 2." The largest of proposed hardwoods within the lot are 4 Live Oak 4'' in DBH situated tightly along several small "green areas" surrounded by concrete pavement.

Mature trees provide benefits that cannot be replicated by replacement plantings for decades. This parcel of land is the last remaining lush green space on the entire stretch of 27 AV up to US1. Various bird species rely on this last space as a source of food and nesting site. Planting "the remainder" in parks in the same district does not replace the localized environmental and community benefits of the existing canopy in this specific location.

The property is located in NCD-3 designated zone, and Chapter 21 recognizes its "historic, heavily landscaped character" aiming to "enhance and protect Coconut Grove's natural features such as the tree canopy and green space." Chapter 17 establishes clear protections regarding the preservation of trees. The intent is to preserve mature trees whenever possible and to ensure that development minimizes removal. The buildable footprint is a design choice, not a fixed constraint.

I respectfully request that the Intended Decision be denied in its entirety, that the developer be required to submit an alternative site plan demonstrating how construction can preserve a greater portion of the existing tree canopy, and that on-site relocation be explored prior to any removal being authorized.

Thank you for your consideration.
Sincerely, an abutting property owner

▶ Example 6 — Abutting property owner (3161 Gifford Ln)

May 11, 2026

To: Preservation Officer and Hearing Boards, City of Miami Environmental Resources, 444 SW 2nd Avenue, Miami, FL 33130

Re: Appeal of Intended Decision - Application No. BD25-008883-001, Subject Property: 3161 Gifford Ln, Date of Intended Decision: May 5, 2026

Dear Preservation Officer and Members of the Hearing Boards,

Pursuant to the Code of the City of Miami, Florida, as amended (the "Code"), I, an abutting property owner, hereby submit this formal appeal of the Intended Decision issued on May 5, 2026, authorizing the removal of seven (7) trees, including three (3) specimen trees located at 3161 Gifford Ln and right-of-way.

As the owner of the directly abutting property, I have a direct and substantial interest in this matter. The trees subject to this Intended Decision are immediately adjacent to my property, and their removal would cause irreversible harm to the shared environment, neighborhood character, and the livability of my home.

The Intended Decision approves tree removal on the basis that the trees are in conflict with proposed construction. Chapter 17 of the City of Miami Code requires that, before removal of any protected or specimen tree is authorized, the applicant must demonstrate that tree preservation was treated as a priority in the site planning process. The application appears to propose tree removals for the convenience of construction and maximizing a development footprint.

The trees proposed for removal are large trees, including three specimen Royal Poinciana trees, an Avocado, a Gumbo Limbo, and an Indian Chestnut. Four of these trees are located in a public right-of-way.

Mature trees of this character provide benefits that cannot be replicated by replacement plantings for decades, including enhancement of neighborhood character, property values, and quality of life, canopy coverage, reduction of the urban heat island effect, air quality improvement, and stormwater management and flood mitigation. These trees represent irreplaceable infrastructure for a resilient, livable city.

To the extent the application proposes replacement with three (3) hardwood trees and a contribution to the Tree Trust Fund, such measures do not adequately compensate for the loss of mature trees. Replacement trees will require decades to provide equivalent ecological or aesthetic value, and the Tree Trust Fund contributions do not replace the localized benefits of the existing canopy.

I respectfully request that the Preservation Officer and Hearing Board review and reconsider this decision and that alternatives be explored that would preserve a greater portion of the trees on the property and all of the trees in the public right-of-way.

Thank you for your consideration.
Sincerely, an abutting property owner

▶ Example 7 — Neighbor / resident (3520 Grand Ave)

March 2026

To: Preservation Officer and Hearing Boards, City of Miami Environmental Resources, 444 SW 2nd Avenue, Miami, FL 33130
Cc: City of Miami

Re: Appeal of Intended Decision, Application No. BD25-012128-001, Location: 3520 Grand Avenue, Miami

Dear Preservation Officer and Members of the Hearing Boards,

I am writing to formally appeal the Intended Decision issued on March 10, 2026 under Application Number BD25-012128-001, concerning the proposed removal, relocation and replacement of trees at 3520 Grand Avenue, Miami. This appeal is submitted pursuant to the Code of the City of Miami, Florida, as amended, and is grounded in two significant and independently dispositive grounds: the proposed tree removal is to accommodate a temporary event that does not justify the permanent loss of mature trees, and the required public notice sign was not posted in a timely manner, effectively depriving nearby residents and neighbors of their right to a meaningful opportunity to respond.

Ground 1: Removal to Accommodate a Temporary Event Permit Does Not Justify Permanent Loss of Mature Trees

The Intended Decision identifies the reason for the proposed tree activity as trees being located in the "buildable footprint." However, upon investigation, the underlying activity is being pursued under a Temporary Event Permit (TE Permit). A Temporary Event Permit is by design an extremely short-term authorization intended to last no more than two weeks, and at most six months even with an extension. In this case, the developer was granted an extension allowing the permit to run for up to six months. Even with that extraordinary extension, a Temporary Event Permit remains a fundamentally impermanent and limited authorization that in no way justifies the permanent destruction of a mature tree canopy. The trees slated for removal include a substantial number of mature, established specimens, three Gumbo Limbo, three Lonkey Pod, two Specimen Lonkey Pod trees, one Specimen Strangler Fig, three Black Olive, two Spanish Lime, two Avocado, and six Palms, among others. Several of these trees carry specimen designation under Chapter 17 of the City of Miami Code, meaning they are afforded the highest level of local protection. It is fundamentally unreasonable and contrary to the purpose and intent of the City's tree protection ordinances to authorize the permanent removal of a large number of mature, protected trees — including specimen trees — in order to accommodate a temporary event that is authorized for only a few weeks under normal circumstances.

Ground 2: Defective Public Notice — Sign Not Posted in a Timely Manner

The Intended Decision was issued on March 10, 2026, with an appeal deadline of March 20, 2026 — a ten-day window during which affected neighbors and residents have the right to review the decision and file an appeal. Proper and timely posting of a public notice sign on the subject property is essential to ensuring that this right is meaningful, not illusory. In this case, no public notice sign was posted on the property at 3520 Grand Avenue at or near the time the Intended Decision was issued on March 10, 2026. The sign was entirely absent from the property for the vast majority of the notice period. Only after a concerned resident made a direct effort to bring this omission to the attention of the City did the property owner post a sign. That sign was dated March 19, 2026, and was placed on the property at approximately 5pm on that date — leaving less than hours before the appeal window closed at 5pm on March 20, 2026. A notice posted for less than one business day, on the eve of the deadline, is not meaningful notice. On this independent procedural ground, the Intended Decision should be denied, or at a minimum, the appeal period should be reset to begin from the date the sign was properly posted.

Conclusion

For the foregoing reasons, we respectfully request that the Intended Decision under Application Number BD25-012128-001 be denied in its entirety, or in the alternative, that the appeal period be extended to allow for proper public participation given the defective notice. The permanent destruction of the tree canopy at 3520 Grand Avenue to benefit a Temporary Event Permit, combined with the failure to provide proper and timely public notice, renders this Intended Decision both substantively unjustifiable and procedurally invalid.

We respectfully urge the Preservation Officer and Hearing Boards to take this appeal seriously and to act in accordance with the letter and spirit of the City's tree protection laws.

Respectfully submitted,
A resident and neighbor
